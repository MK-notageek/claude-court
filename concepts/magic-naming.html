The MAGIC Naming Formula Why Names Convert — or Kill

The Name Is the First Filter

Names convert — or they vanish.

A generic name forces the prospect to project their own meaning onto your offer. They rarely project anything useful.

When someone sees "Business Coaching — 3 Months," they think: is this for me? They don't know. So they move on. A precise name does the qualification before a single conversation has happened. The right buyer reads it and leans in. The wrong buyer self-selects out — which is equally valuable.

The MAGIC Naming Formula The Five Letters

Framework Overview

Five layers. One irresistible name.

MAGIC is not an acronym you memorize — it's an assembly line for building names that sell themselves.

Each letter adds a distinct dimension of clarity to your offer name. Stack all five and you have a name that communicates who it's for, what they get, by when, and how — in a single phrase.

You don't need all five in every name. But the more layers you include, the more the name does the selling before you ever speak. Think of it as compressing your entire sales pitch into eight words.

Five layers of specificity — each one narrowing the right buyer in while filtering the wrong buyer out.

The MAGIC Naming Formula M — Magnetic Reason Why

M · Magnetic Reason Why

Give urgency a narrative peg.

The reason why answers a silent question the prospect is already asking: "Why would I need this right now?"

It doesn't have to be a major world event. It can be a season, a platform shift, a life stage, or a trend. "For the AI era," "for the post-Covid pivot," "for the iOS14 update" — each one makes the offer feel timed rather than timeless. That urgency is the difference between "I'll look at this later" and "I should look at this now."

Importantly, the reason why doesn't need to be unique to you. If the market is living through a shift, name it. You're not inventing the urgency — you're acknowledging it, and offering a solution.

Adding a timely reason why transforms a passive interest into active, motivated urgency.

The MAGIC Naming Formula A — Avatar

A · Avatar

The name pre-qualifies your leads.

The more precisely you name the person, the better the leads you attract — and the worse the leads you repel.

"Entrepreneurs" is not a target. "First-time SaaS founders between $0 and $30k MRR" is a target. When the right person reads it, they feel seen. When the wrong person reads it, they move on — exactly as intended.

Fear of specificity is the most common naming mistake. The instinct is to keep it broad — "coaches, consultants, and course creators" — to cast the widest net. But broad names catch broad results: low conversion, long sales cycles, and buyers who were never right for you. Precision is not exclusion. It's accuracy.

Specificity is not exclusion. It is accuracy. Precise avatar + precise outcome = your best clients find you.

The MAGIC Naming Formula G + I — Goal & Interval

G · Goal    I · Interval

Specific outcomes. Defined windows.

The goal makes the result imaginable. The interval makes it believable. Together they form a promise that feels both real and achievable.

G — Goal: "Grow your business" is not a goal. "Add $50k in new ARR" is. The more specific the outcome, the more easily the prospect can picture themselves experiencing it. Imagination precedes desire.

I — Interval: "In 90 days" is more credible than "fast." A defined time window also implies a system — because random effort doesn't have a timeline. If you can name the window, you're signaling that you've run this process before and know how long it actually takes.

Goal + Interval = a promise the brain can picture and the gut can believe. Vagueness kills desire.

The MAGIC Naming Formula C — Container

C · Container

The vehicle shapes the perception.

The container word is the product type — and it carries more perception than most founders realize.

"Protocol" signals rigor. "Sprint" signals intensity and pace. "Bootcamp" signals transformation through challenge. "Inner Circle" signals exclusivity and peer proximity. The word you choose positions your offer against every other thing in the market, before anyone reads the description.

The container also sets expectations for the experience, the peer group, and the level of access. "A course" competes with every $97 Udemy option in the category. "The Intensive" does not. Container choice is a pricing signal as much as a product signal.

Container words are price anchors. The right word instantly repositions your offer out of commodity pricing.

The MAGIC Naming Formula The Takeaway

A name is not decoration.

The name is the first sales conversation your offer has — without you in the room. When it names the exact person, the exact outcome, and the exact time frame, the right prospect reads it and thinks: "This is for me." The wrong prospect moves on. Both outcomes are wins.

You don't need a clever name. You need a precise one. MAGIC gives you the assembly line: a hook to make it timely, a person to make it personal, a goal to make it desired, a window to make it credible, and a container to make it positioned. Run the formula. Name the offer. Then deliver.
