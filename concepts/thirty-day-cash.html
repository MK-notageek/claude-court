01
THE 30-DAY CASH MODELWhy Profit Lies

You can be profitable. And still broke.

Revenue on an invoice is a promise. Cash in the account is oxygen. The two numbers diverge the moment you let anyone pay you later.

A $200,000 annual contract looks identical whether clients pay on signing or quarterly. But one model means you personally cover payroll in March while waiting on Q2 cheques.

The business that earns revenue and the business that controls cash are not the same business. Only one of them survives a bad month.

02
THE 30-DAY CASH MODELThe Timing Trap

Same contract. Three different realities.

Take a $120,000 annual contract. Paid quarterly: $30,000 every 90 days. Paid monthly: $10,000 every 30 days. Paid upfront on signing: $120,000 hits the account before you lift a finger.

Three identical agreements. Three radically different balance sheets. The work never changes. Only the timing does.

03
THE 30-DAY CASH MODELThree Models

Three ways to collect the same dollar.

Most businesses default to whatever the client asks for — or whatever is standard in the industry. That is a mistake. You choose the model that serves your cash position first.

Each model has legitimate uses, but they are not equivalent. Annual prepaid is better in almost every situation where the client will accept it.

04
THE 30-DAY CASH MODELThe Big Lever

Annual upfront. The most underused lever in business.

Monthly billing at $1,000/month gives you $12,000 over 12 months. Annual upfront at $10,000 — a 17% discount — gives you $10,000 on day one.

You give up $2,000. You get back 12 months of operational certainty, zero churn exposure, and the full float period. With 100 clients, the difference on January 1st is $910,000 in the bank versus $100,000.

17% cheaper to the client.
12× more valuable to you.
05
THE 30-DAY CASH MODELThe Float

Money you collect before you spend it is working capital.

Float is the gap between when you collect and when you spend. Airlines, insurance companies, and banks have built trillion-dollar businesses on float alone. Your business has the same lever.

Pre-payment lets you fund delivery with client money — not debt, not equity. A 30-day intensive sold upfront is a zero-interest line of working capital.

The float does not require you to speculate — even a high-yield business account earns something. But the real value is operational: you hire, build, and deliver without needing a bridge loan.

06
THE 30-DAY CASH MODELThe Sprint Offer

Design the offer around the 30-day window.

A 30-day intensive — a sprint, cohort, bootcamp, or immersion — is the cleanest delivery vehicle for upfront billing. One price. One payment. Collected before day one.

It is also renewable: the same client can buy the next cohort. Unlike a year-long retainer, it has a defined end that makes it easier for the buyer to say yes. Short commitment, big result.

The sprint also solves the fulfilment problem: 30 days of intensive work produces more client results than 12 months of loose monthly access. Better outcomes drive referrals.

07
THE 30-DAY CASH MODELCompound It

Each cohort stacks. The base never resets.

The first cohort is your smallest cash event. Every cohort after adds to the base without replacing it. Existing annual clients renew; new cohorts pile on top.

Ten new clients per month at $10,000 each. Month one: $100,000. Month six: $600,000 in annual contracts. Month twelve: $1.2M ARR — even with flat, linear growth.

The effect compounds once renewals kick in. Cohort 1 clients renewing at month 13 add a second revenue event on top of all the cohorts that followed them. The math gets better every quarter.

08
THE 30-DAY CASH MODELThree Conversations

Three decisions. One conversation each.

You do not need to rebuild your business. You need three conversations: move existing clients from monthly to annual, move payment from post-delivery to pre-delivery, move your 1:1 work into 30-day cohort formats.

✗“I’ll send an invoice at the end of each month after our sessions.”
✓“Here’s the 30-day intensive. One payment before we kick off — and it’s $X less than monthly.”
