THE ATTRACTION OFFERThe Foundation

Not a free lead magnet. Not your core offer.

The attraction offer lives in the gap between “free” and “expensive” — close enough to free to eliminate friction, but priced high enough to filter for people who actually buy.

A $0 lead magnet collects email addresses. A $7–$97 attraction offer collects buyers. The psychological distance between giving an email address and handing over a credit card is enormous — crossing it signals intent that no opt-in ever can.

02
THE ATTRACTION OFFERThe Three Criteria

Every attraction offer must pass three tests.

One purpose, three requirements. Miss any one and you’ve accidentally built a lead magnet (too free), a core offer (too expensive), or a charity (doesn’t fund itself).

The three criteria work together: the right price attracts the right people, a specific promise filters the wrong ones out, and the volume of buyers funds the cost of getting them. All three, or start over.

03
THE ATTRACTION OFFERPricing Psychology

Free gets you everybody. Paid gets you buyers.

Here’s the paradox: a higher price can increase the quality of responses when you’re selecting for the right audience. Charging $47 for something you could give away filters out the people who cost you the most in refunds, support tickets, and complaints.

Hormozi’s framing: “The dream customer shows up with credit card in hand. The nightmare customer shows up asking for discounts.” The price is the first filter. Use it deliberately.

04
THE ATTRACTION OFFERCommitment & Identity

Paying creates investment.

Behavioral science is consistent: people value what they pay for. A $97 course sees 60% completion; the same course given free gets 3%. The act of paying transforms a passive browser into an active participant with a stake in the outcome.

And once someone has bought from you once — even $7 — the barrier to the next purchase drops by more than half. The first transaction is the hardest. Every one after is downhill because they’ve already decided you’re worth trusting.

05
THE ATTRACTION OFFERThe Math

Your ad spend pays for itself.

If it costs $50 to generate a lead and your $97 attraction offer converts at 60%, you spend $50 and immediately recoup $58. Customer acquisition cost: near zero. The leads are now effectively free — you’ve built a self-liquidating funnel.

The AO doesn’t need to make you rich. It needs to break even. The profit lives downstream — your core offer, your continuity, your high-ticket backend. The AO funds the machine that fills them all.

06
THE ATTRACTION OFFERThe Filter In Action

The right offer repels as hard as it attracts.

A well-built attraction offer is a two-way filter. It draws in serious buyers and actively repels browsers, discount-seekers, and the chronically discontent — people who exhaust your team and churn loudest.

✗Free 5-day challenge — 3,000 sign-ups, 180 show up, 12 ever buy anything.
✓$47 workshop — 200 sign-ups, 190 show up, 80 upgrade to the core offer.
✗More leads always means more revenue. (False. More unqualified leads means more cost.)
✓Fewer, better leads. The right 200 outperform the wrong 3,000 every time.
07
THE ATTRACTION OFFERThe Architecture

The attraction offer is just the door.

The AO gets them in. Everything else gets them to stay and spend. Convert a stranger to a buyer (AO), solve their core problem (Core Offer), then keep them winning month after month (Continuity).

Hormozi’s rule: the money is made on the second sale, not the first. Most businesses try to profit on the first sale and lose future customers to friction. The AO inverts this — it gives value first, earns the relationship, and lets the backend do the heavy lifting.

08
THE ATTRACTION OFFERThe Build

Pick one problem. Price the access.

Start with one micro-problem your ideal customer has today. Not their biggest problem — the one at the surface. The one keeping them up last night. Solve that one thing completely, at a price they can say yes to in under ten seconds.

Three non-negotiables: fast to consume (under two hours), immediately actionable (they see a win within 24 hours), specific promise (one outcome, not “transformation”). The wider the promise, the lower the conversion. Own a lane.

One problem.
One promise.
One price.
09
THE ATTRACTION OFFERShip It

Ship one. Measure. Iterate.

You’re not optimizing in theory. Ship a product, run it to $1,000 in revenue, then ask: did it cover its own acquisition cost? Did buyers upgrade? The math either works or you adjust one variable.

Most businesses skip the AO entirely, spending every dollar driving cold traffic straight to their core offer and wondering why CAC is crippling margins. Build the door first. The house follows.
