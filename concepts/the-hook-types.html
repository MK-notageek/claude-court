THE ANATOMY OF A HOOKWhy It Matters

The hook isn’t the start — it’s 80%.

100 people scroll past your content. If 70 keep scrolling, you’ve already lost the game — before a single word of your argument is read.

The funnel is a cliff. The hook controls whether anyone falls off the top or keeps reading. Improve the hook and everything downstream improves with it.

Great content with a weak hook = a full restaurant no one walks into. Great hook with decent content = sold out every night.

02
THE ANATOMY OF A HOOKThe Four Parts

Four jobs. Three seconds.

Every hook that consistently works does four things in sequence. Skip any one and the chain breaks.

Pattern Interrupt: snaps them out of the trance. Promise: answers “what’s in it for me?” Proof Indicator: answers “why should I believe you?” Curiosity Gap: makes them need to know what’s next.

Not every hook makes all four explicit. But the best ones hit all four implicitly — often in a single sentence.

03
THE ANATOMY OF A HOOKPart 1 — Pattern Interrupt

The scroll is a trance. Break it.

Every platform trains users to consume automatically, without thinking. Your first job is to introduce a speed bump — something unexpected, emotionally charged, or visually jarring enough to trigger a conscious pause.

Three ways to interrupt the pattern: unexpected contrast (say the opposite of what they expect), high-specificity numbers (“$14,397” not “a lot”), or naked vulnerability (“I was broke at 32.”).

Predictable = invisible. Unexpected = a pause. A pause is all you need.

04
THE ANATOMY OF A HOOKPart 2 — The Promise

Specificity converts. Vagueness repels.

Once you’ve stopped them, you have one second to answer: what’s in it for me? Generic answers lose them again instantly. Specific numbers and named outcomes hold them.

The formula: concrete result + specific timeframe + zero-cost element. “I added $23K per month in 6 weeks without running a single ad.” Every word does work.

✗“How to make more money in your business.”
✓“How I added $23,400/mo in 6 weeks — without paid ads.”
05
THE ANATOMY OF A HOOKThe Six Archetypes

Six structures that always work.

Not every hook type works for every audience or platform. But these six show up consistently in top-performing content across channels — master all six and rotate based on what the market responds to.

Rotate the archetype; keep the specificity. A Curiosity Gap hook that’s vague still dies. Specificity is the override that makes any archetype convert.

06
THE ANATOMY OF A HOOKPart 4 — The Curiosity Gap

Open the loop. Never close it.

The mind hates open loops. It’s wired to resolve tension. A hook that reveals just enough to create a gap between what they know and what they need to know becomes irresistible.

The gap formula: tell them what exists, not what it is. “The one thing 97% of founders get wrong about hiring” — the gap is the thing. Don’t fill it.

TEASE THE GAP. THE CONTENT FILLS IT.

07
THE ANATOMY OF A HOOKThe WHO — Specificity as a Magnet

Naming the “for who” narrows in.

This seems backwards: the tighter you define your audience, the more people feel spoken to. “For everyone” resonates with no one. “For agency owners stuck between $10K–$50K/mo” makes the right person stop dead.

The exclusion is the attraction. People who don’t fit the description still read it out of curiosity. People who do fit feel like you read their mind.

Specificity signals expertise. You can only write that precisely about someone if you’ve worked with dozens of them.

08
THE ANATOMY OF A HOOKThe 100 Hooks Practice

Write 100 hooks. Test 3. Scale 1.

One hook idea isn’t a strategy. The only way to find what works is volume. Write 100 variations on your concept, then run the top 10 by your best customer or a sharp colleague. Post the top 3. Scale the one that wins.

Hormozi’s rule: spend as much time on the hook as the content itself. If the content took two hours, the hook deserves two hours.

✗Write content. Pick the first hook that comes to mind. Post.
✓List 100 hook variations. Filter to 3. Post. Track. Double down on the winner.
