TRIM & STACKFrom Diverge to Converge

A hundred options. Eight become your offer.

After the divergent list, you are staring at too many solutions. That is exactly right. The trim is the moment an offer is born.

Not everything survives — and that is the point. A Grand Slam Offer is not a list of everything you can do. It is the specific combination that creates maximum perceived value at minimum cost to deliver.

The divergent list was about width. This phase is about depth — running a filter over every idea and keeping only the ones that belong in the offer.

02
TRIM & STACKThe Trim Grid

Two axes. One clear decision.

Every solution on your list gets placed on a simple grid. The two axes are the only questions that matter:

How much does the customer value this? If they heard about it, would they want it? Score high or low.

How much does it cost you to deliver? Time, money, team, operational complexity. Score high or low.

The only quadrant you want is high-value, low-cost. That is your offer. Everything else is a distraction or a charity project.

03
TRIM & STACKScoring the Solutions

Ask two questions per item.

Question one: “If a prospect heard about this, would they want it badly?” Rate the pull it would have in a sales conversation. 1 = irrelevant, 10 = changes the whole decision.

Question two: “How expensive is this to deliver?” Include time, materials, labour, attention, and operational complexity. 1 = free forever, 10 = full-time employee.

You want high on the first, low on the second. Solutions that score 8+ on value and 3 or below on cost are your stack candidates. Everything else waits.

04
TRIM & STACKThe Stack

The survivors become the stack.

A stack is not a list — it is an architecture. The core promise is the hero. Each surviving solution is a supporting cast member that solves one specific objection around the main offer.

A good stack has one item per fear point. No time? Here’s the system. No skills? Here’s the training. Won’t work for me? Here are the case studies. Not worth it? Here’s the template pack.

The buyer works through each item mentally, thinking: “That handles X.” By the end, all their objections have been addressed — in advance.

05
TRIM & STACKThe Psychology

The gap between price and perceived value.

The buyer does not calculate ROI. They compare how much they feel they’re getting against the number on the screen. The stack engineering that gap.

As each bonus is announced with its standalone value, the buyer builds a mental total. By the time the price appears, their internal tally is already three to five times higher.

06
TRIM & STACKDelivery Mechanism Variants

The same solution, four delivery forms.

When a solution survives the trim, the next question is how to deliver it. Each form has a different cost-to-value ratio. Some that look expensive become cheap when delivered at scale. Some that seem free take more time than they are worth.

The golden rule: use the delivery form that gives the buyer the highest perceived value while costing you the least ongoing time and money. A live call that replaces a $2,000 hire is a bargain. A checklist PDF that answers the number-one question is worth ten coaching sessions.

07
TRIM & STACKThe Presentation

Build up slowly. Drop the price last.

The sequence of your reveal matters as much as the stack itself. Announce each component, name a standalone value, let it land, then add the next. Each item stacks on the last.

The price appears only when the mental tally is at its peak. The moment you say the price, the buyer compares it to the total they’ve been building in their head — not to market rates.

Never mention the price until every item has been presented. And never summarise before the price — let each item sit in isolation first, then let the total hit.

08
TRIM & STACKBuild It Now

The offer is never just the product.

Score every solution from your divergent list. Keep only high-value, low-cost. Bundle them with intention. Present in the right order. Watch the price feel smaller than it did yesterday.
